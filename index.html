Overview

These photographs and descriptions of the Prague National Technical Museum were inscribed by attendees of the International Communication Association's mobile preconference (ICA Mobile, www.icamobile.org) on May 23, 2018. The group held its annual conference at the museum and experimented with location-based media and inscribing "en plein air" as part of an exercise conducted by an international team of scholars (Brett Oppegaard, University of Hawaii, U.S.; Ana Rita Morais, Ryerson University, Canada; Jordan Frith, University of North Texas, U.S.; and Ivar John Erdal and Kjetil Oie, Volda University College, Norway).

From first motorcycles to old planes and helicopters

There are a few old planes and helicopters in this museum, which we may relate to the human need of moving around and communicating with one another. Besides, there are antiques and the first prototypes of motorcycles. They used to be common bicycles with a simple assembly of a combustion engine on the frame. The one I am looking at right now, as I write this description, is dark green, and it is called "Slávia B." It was made back in 1901 by Laurin & Klement, Mladá Boleslav, Čechy. It is interesting to think about how these modes of transportation allowed people to get together and experience places differently. Now I am looking at a photo of a couple happily having a picnic in a park alongside their motorcycle.

Large black locomotive

This is a massive engine from an earlier era of trains. It was made in 1911. Standing beside it, you feel dwarfed by its height (three times yours). The wheels only are taller than you. You can almost feel the weight of the 86 tonnes.

Locomotive

If you are standing underneath the red plane, you are nearby or facing a large black locomotive from 1911. This locomotive is old and really heavy. It used to ride on railroads, which is really, really long. It is hard to see from the ground, but the wagon carries bricks of coal.

First car

Among the first cars, drivers had to manipulate multiple hand controls, in addition to steering the car with the wheel. Multiple levers extend on the right side of the drive, moving up and down. Additional levers are in front of the driver around the wheel. Together these control the breaks, gear, and clutch. A foot pedal in front of the driver engages the speed.

Electric iron

A variety of previously unavailable foreign goods were available for purchase in Czechoslovakia at Tuzex stores. High-tech products, such as electric irons, were in great demand because it reduced the amount of time required for household tasks, which opened up time for use in other areas such as self-development, education, and family. This yellow iron – made of hard plastic and able to be filled with water – allowed its user to be able to iron from any area of the house, which in turn allowed for greater participation in household activities and the ability to multitask with communication technologies, such as television or radio.

Skirt Washroom/Restroom

This is the door to the skirt restroom/toilet. The handle is well designed for accessibility because it is not a knob. However, while the door is divided down the middle, the second door does not automatically open. There is no button for automatic opening, which means that wheel chair users may not find it easy to enter without assistance. On the door is an iconic figure of someone in a skirt, the other indicates that there is a table for changing a diaper. Those who do not wear skirts may use the door on the opposite side of the hallway. You may also change a baby’s diaper in this room. We have not tested this feature. The signage on the door Indicates how this door might function as a ‘technology of gender’, dividing those with skirts from those without. You must decide if you are female or male, as indicated by these signs. But perhaps you wear the pants and may decide to enter, performing a small act of performative resistance. Perhaps you resist gendered identities. Or don’t fit into these binaries. Even better.
